Syllabus

The weekly plan distinguishes available materials from planned topics. Each week groups the resources students use for that week; planned topics are not publication claims. Return to the course overview or browse past offerings and recordings.

Week 1 — K-anonymity

Date: 2026-10-20 Status: Forthcoming

Goal: Begin with modern and historical privacy failures, then examine k-anonymity as a plausible first defense. Composition, homogeneous groups, and auxiliary information show why hiding identity is not the same as hiding sensitive facts—and why the course needs a stronger adversarial definition.

Week 2 — Reconstruction attacks

Date: 2026-10-27 Status: Available

Goal: See how accurate answers to many aggregate questions become constraints on a hidden database. Brute force, least squares, and linear programming reveal both why reconstruction is possible and why enough defensive noise is unavoidable.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 3 — DP as hypothesis testing

Date: 2026-11-03 Status: Available

Goal: Model privacy leakage as the strongest adversary’s attempt to distinguish two neighboring datasets. Likelihood ratios, ROC curves, and decision rules lead to an operational interpretation of epsilon and delta rather than a definition presented without motivation.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides · Exercise ·

Week 4 — Privacy auditing

Date: 2026-11-10 Status: Available

Goal: Reverse the hypothesis-testing view of differential privacy: use sampled outputs and a fixed distinguishing event to obtain an empirical lower bound on privacy loss. Confidence intervals show why an apparent attack must be corrected for finite-sample uncertainty.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 5 — Private estimation

Date: 2026-11-17 Status: Available

Goal: Estimate means, medians, quantiles, and histograms while auditing tempting but invalid shortcuts. The central lesson is that useful privacy often comes from changing the utility measure or privatizing data-dependent choices—not simply adding noise to an ordinary estimator.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 6 — Private Subgroup Comparisons After Clipping

Date: 2026-11-24 Status: Available

Goal: Once the value range is controlled, subgroup analysis becomes a problem of denominators and data-dependent stability. Compare global sensitivity, invalid oracle local sensitivity, noisy counts and sums, propose-test-release, and smooth sensitivity, then introduce private selection and thresholding.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 7 — Private linear regression

Date: 2026-12-01 Status: Available

Goal: Move from private scalar statistics to a private model. Regression geometry, leverage, and conditioning explain why input and output perturbation can fail, while noisy sufficient statistics and regularization show where privacy noise can be injected more effectively.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 8 — MNIST linear-query classifier

Date: 2026-12-08 Status: Available

Goal: Build a visual classifier whose weights are themselves a bounded vector-valued query. A model can look overwhelmed by privacy noise while still predicting accurately, motivating effective dimension and the distinction between population-level signal, generalization, and privacy leakage.

Materials: Self-study notes · · Slides

Week 9 — Machine-learning privacy attacks and auditing

Date: 2026-12-15 Status: Planned

Goal: Use logistic-regression losses and margins to ask whether a trained model behaves differently because a particular record was included. Typical and worst-case membership audits reconnect machine learning to the ROC and confidence-bound tools developed earlier in the course.

Notes: Planned — materials forthcoming.

Week 10 — DP-SGD, stability, and generalization

Date: 2026-12-22 Status: Planned

Goal: Open the iterative training loop and identify each example’s gradient contribution. Per-example clipping, calibrated Gaussian noise, subsampling, and composition produce DP-SGD; re-auditing the trained model tests whether the repair controls the worst-case influence exposed previously.

Notes: Planned — materials forthcoming.

Week 11 — Private parametric synthesis

Date: 2027-01-05 Status: Planned

Goal: Study synthetic data as post-processing of private measurements, not as privacy by imitation. Begin with a transparent parametric example and expose the difference between mechanism error and model misspecification.

Notes: Planned — materials forthcoming.

Week 12 — Marginals are not a dataset

Date: 2027-01-12 Status: Planned

Goal: See why accurate one-way marginals can still erase the relationships an analysis needs. The lecture introduces workload-aware utility through correlations and selected two-way measurements.

Notes: Planned — materials forthcoming.

Week 13 — Workload-aware synthetic data

Date: 2027-01-19 Status: Planned

Goal: Confront the product-domain cost of a full joint histogram, then design a generator around a declared set of analytical questions and honest held-out evaluation.

Notes: Planned — materials forthcoming.

Week 14 — Course synthesis and project presentations

Date: 2027-01-26 Status: Planned

Goal: Bring the course’s central question together: how can useful learning limit any one person’s influence? Students compare attacks, mechanisms, utility assumptions, and open limitations.

Notes: Planned — format and materials forthcoming.
