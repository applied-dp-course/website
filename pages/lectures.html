Lectures and self-study notes

The lectures form one cumulative investigation of individual influence and useful learning from sensitive data. Each topic begins with an attack, tempting proposal, or statistical problem; makes the failure visible through a small example or experiment; and develops the formal privacy tool needed to repair it. Use the self-study notes for full explanations and executable examples, and the slides for the shorter classroom narrative. Planned topics are listed for orientation but do not yet have published materials.

K-anonymity

Week 1

Status: Forthcoming

Begin with modern and historical privacy failures, then examine k-anonymity as a plausible first defense. Composition, homogeneous groups, and auxiliary information show why hiding identity is not the same as hiding sensitive facts—and why the course needs a stronger adversarial definition.

Reconstruction Attacks

Week 2

Status: Available

See how accurate answers to many aggregate questions become constraints on a hidden database. Brute force, least squares, and linear programming reveal both why reconstruction is possible and why enough defensive noise is unavoidable.

DP as Hypothesis Testing

Week 3

Status: Available

Model privacy leakage as the strongest adversary’s attempt to distinguish two neighboring datasets. Likelihood ratios, ROC curves, and decision rules lead to an operational interpretation of epsilon and delta rather than a definition presented without motivation.

Privacy Auditing

Week 4

Status: Available

Reverse the hypothesis-testing view of differential privacy: use sampled outputs and a fixed distinguishing event to obtain an empirical lower bound on privacy loss. Confidence intervals show why an apparent attack must be corrected for finite-sample uncertainty.

Private Estimation

Week 5

Status: Available

Estimate means, medians, quantiles, and histograms while auditing tempting but invalid shortcuts. The central lesson is that useful privacy often comes from changing the utility measure or privatizing data-dependent choices—not simply adding noise to an ordinary estimator.

Private Subgroup Comparisons After Clipping

Week 6

Status: Available

Once the value range is controlled, subgroup analysis becomes a problem of denominators and data-dependent stability. Compare global sensitivity, invalid oracle local sensitivity, noisy counts and sums, propose-test-release, and smooth sensitivity, then introduce private selection and thresholding.

Private linear regression

Week 7

Status: Available

Move from private scalar statistics to a private model. Regression geometry, leverage, and conditioning explain why input and output perturbation can fail, while noisy sufficient statistics and regularization show where privacy noise can be injected more effectively.

MNIST linear-query classifier

Week 8

Status: Available

Build a visual classifier whose weights are themselves a bounded vector-valued query. A model can look overwhelmed by privacy noise while still predicting accurately, motivating effective dimension and the distinction between population-level signal, generalization, and privacy leakage.

Machine-learning privacy attacks and auditing

Week 9

Status: Planned

Use logistic-regression losses and margins to ask whether a trained model behaves differently because a particular record was included. Typical and worst-case membership audits reconnect machine learning to the ROC and confidence-bound tools developed earlier in the course.

DP-SGD, stability, and generalization

Week 10

Status: Planned

Open the iterative training loop and identify each example’s gradient contribution. Per-example clipping, calibrated Gaussian noise, subsampling, and composition produce DP-SGD; re-auditing the trained model tests whether the repair controls the worst-case influence exposed previously.

Private parametric synthesis

Week 11

Status: Planned

Study synthetic data as post-processing of private measurements, not as privacy by imitation. Begin with a transparent parametric example and expose the difference between mechanism error and model misspecification.

Marginals are not a dataset

Week 12

Status: Planned

See why accurate one-way marginals can still erase the relationships an analysis needs. The lecture introduces workload-aware utility through correlations and selected two-way measurements.

Workload-aware synthetic data

Week 13

Status: Planned

Confront the product-domain cost of a full joint histogram, then design a generator around a declared set of analytical questions and honest held-out evaluation.

Course synthesis and project presentations

Week 14

Status: Planned

Bring the course’s central question together: how can useful learning limit any one person’s influence? Students compare attacks, mechanisms, utility assumptions, and open limitations.
